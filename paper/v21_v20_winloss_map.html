Where reasoning through the steering flow pays

Every 2020+ WP/EP test storm, drawn once and coloured by which model forecast it better. Blue means the chain-of-thought model (v21), which predicts the steering flow and derives the track from it, beat v20 on that storm; red means it lost. Thick lines are the 18 storms where the two models differ by more than 100 km — they carry more than half the entire average gain.

The chain-of-thought structure is what earns this, not the auxiliary task. A control arm that supervises the same flow head but does NOT feed it into the motion scores 452.91 km — identical to v20. Only when the track is actually derived from the predicted flow does the error fall, to 443.62 km. So the gain belongs to the structure, not to multi-task regularisation.

It is also broader-based than v20's gain was: v21 wins 80 of 144 storms and the median storm improves by 5 km, where v20 beat v17 on only 71 and had a median of −0.5. But a paired-storm bootstrap still returns −8.85 km with a 95% CI of [−18.30, +1.31] — it misses significance by 1.31 km. Evidence, not proof.

Mean track latitude is 22.3°N for the storms v21 wins and 24.4°N for the ones it loses; peak latitude averages 28.5°N against 32.4°N. Read the map before trusting any story those numbers suggest: interleaved colours mean variance, spatial clustering means physics.

Tracks are the observed best-track positions of each storm's forecast windows, so a line is the path the storm actually took, not a forecast. Colour encodes the mean over every full-horizon forecast that storm produced, so long-lived storms are scored on more forecasts than brief ones.
