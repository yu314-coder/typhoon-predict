TrackFormer · forensic review

v9 → v15: what each model ate, how it was trained, and why v14 “broke”

Six models share one dataset and one training recipe. They differ in two things: how the persistence baseline is drawn, and what environmental field is fed to the track decoder. That second difference is where the story is.

Verdict

v14 is not a bad model. It is the best model in the lineage — and its one catastrophic result is an artifact of corrupted input data, not a defect in the network.

v14 beats every predecessor on aggregate test error and has the tightest tail on the 2020+ test set. Its 3,442 km blow-up on Bavi 2026 traces to a silent bug in the steering-field extractor that fed it a physically impossible wind field for that year. v14.1 and v15 were both designed to fix a problem that does not exist as diagnosed.

Root cause

The failure chain

Each step below was measured, not inferred. The numbering is the causal order — every link was tested before moving to the next.

  1. The failure is one storm, not a pattern

    v14 improves on Wayne, Co-may and Hinnamnor, and wins on every aggregate statistic including p90 and max. Only Bavi 2026 regresses — from ~640 km to 3,442 km.

  2. It is a direction reversal, not a magnitude error

    At +120 h, Bavi's true displacement is 2,029 km west and 1,213 km north. v14 predicts 1,360 km east — a bearing error of 106°. For comparison v10's bearing error is 1° and v13's is −12°. v14 sends the storm the wrong way across the basin.

  3. Its steering input says “strong westerly” when the storm went west

    Mean 500 hPa zonal wind over the inner 5×5 of Bavi's patches is +10.75 m/s (eastward flow). The training-set average for WP storms is +0.88 m/s. v14 feeds steering into the track decoder with no regularization, so it followed the field — correctly, given what it was told.

  4. Every 2026 storm is affected, so it isn't weather

    Year-mean u500 for WP storms runs between +0.03 and +3.09 m/s for every year from 2008 to 2025 (mean +1.67, sd 0.90). 2026 comes out at +9.01 m/s — an 8.2σ outlier. Steering climatology does not move that far; a data pipeline does.

  5. The extractor matches times with no bounds check

    NCEP fields are matched to storm windows by nearest timestamp. argmin always returns an index — when a window falls past the end of the file, it silently snaps to the last available day instead of failing.

    # extract_steer.py — per window k
ti = int(np.abs(tns - bt[k]).argmin())   ← no tolerance, no bounds check
li = int(np.abs(lat - bla[k]).argmin())
lj = int(np.abs(lon - (blo[k] % 360.0)).argmin())

    2026 is the current year, so its reanalysis file is partial. Every storm after the file's last timestep received that timestep's field.

  6. Confirmed: duplicate patches months apart

    Taking pairs of windows in the same 2.5° grid cell more than 30 days apart, and comparing their steering patches byte-for-byte:

    YearPairsIdentical patch

    202644443.0%

    Two storms two months apart cannot share an identical wind field. 2025 shows zero such collisions; 2026 shows 43%.

  7. Therefore the Bavi number measures the data, not the model

    2026 sits in the test split, so training was never contaminated — only evaluation was. v10 and v13 are unaffected because neither consumes u500. v14's “blow-up” is the correct response to a corrupted input.

Consequence

What this means for v14.1 and v15

v14.1 was built to stop the model over-trusting steering — dropout on the whole field plus 4σ clipping. Measured against v14 it cuts Bavi from 3,442 to 2,257 km, but it cannot repair an input that is simply wrong, and it pays for the attempt: aggregate error rises 546 → 561 and the worst case on the test set gets substantially worse, 4,329 → 5,828 km. It trades real accuracy for partial cover over a data bug.

v15 — training on Colab as of this writing — adds equatorial-mirror augmentation. That is a sound idea on its own merits: 34% of the dataset is Southern Hemisphere, and mirroring lets every storm teach both hemispheres. But it does nothing about 2026 steering, so the Bavi number will not improve, and if it's read as the headline result the experiment will look like a failure for the wrong reason.

The actual fix is three lines in extract_steer.py: reject any window whose nearest reanalysis timestamp is more than ~12 h away, mark those windows as steering-unavailable, and let the model's existing steering-dropout path handle them as missing rather than as fabricated data. Then re-evaluate v14 on Bavi before drawing any further conclusions about steering.

Inputs

How the data is built

Source A — best track

IBTrACS v04r01, all basins, downloaded from NOAA NCEI. 4,264 storms, 1980–2026, 6-hourly fixes.

Source B — steering

NCEP/NCAR reanalysis daily means. Storm-centred 17×17 patches on a 2.5° grid (±20°), 500 hPa level.

Window

9 history steps × 54 features. Split into kinematic, thermodynamic and environmental streams that are encoded separately.

Split

By each storm's first year, so no storm crosses a boundary. Train ≤2015 · valid 2016–19 · test ≥2020.

The 54 input features

  • Kinematic — position deltas, speed, heading sin/cos, turn rate. Routed to the track decoder with its own gradients; the intensity head only sees a detached copy.
  • Thermodynamic — vmax, central pressure, gust, radius of maximum wind, the twelve wind radii, validity flags. Routed to the intensity decoder; reaches track only through a zero-initialised gated adapter.
  • Environmental (columns 48–53) — latitude, |latitude|, sin/cos longitude, distance to land, and a latitude×month climatological SST proxy. Routed to both decoders.

The 17 outputs, at each of 20 lead times

  • 0:2 — east and north displacement in km, stored as per-step increments; the track is their cumulative sum. Predicting increments rather than absolute positions is what keeps long-horizon error from compounding through the decoder.
  • 2:5 — vmax, central pressure, radius of maximum wind.
  • 5:17 — twelve wind radii: {34, 50, 64 kt} × {NE, SE, SW, NW}.

Leads run +6 h to +120 h in 6-hour steps. A boolean mask marks which targets actually exist, so missing radii and storms that end early contribute nothing to the loss rather than teaching zeros.

The loss has two parts. Position is scored with smooth-L1 on the cumulative track, so the model is penalised for where the storm ends up rather than for each increment in isolation. Everything else is a masked heteroscedastic Gaussian negative log-likelihood: a second head emits a log-scale per target, so the network states its own uncertainty and is not forced to be confident about quantities it cannot resolve.

Targets are rescaled to comparable magnitudes before the loss ([100, 100, 35, 20, 50, 50×12]) so that a kilometre of track error and a knot of vmax error carry sensible relative weight.
