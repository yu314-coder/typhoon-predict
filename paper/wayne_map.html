Typhoon Wayne: the loop the model can't see coming.

Wayne (1986) is the most erratic Western Pacific typhoon on record — 21 days, several full loops. The amber line is its complete observed path. The model's single 120-hour forecast, issued early, is in cyan. It has no way to anticipate a reversal that hasn't happened yet — and it doesn't.

The forecast is good for 24 h (115 km), then diverges hard — 567 km by 48 h, over 1,000 km by 120 h. The model reads Wayne's current heading and extrapolates; the storm loops back on itself instead.

Wayne is in the training set — and it still fails. That's the key lesson: this isn't a memorization gap. A loop is driven by the surrounding environment reorganizing, which best-track history simply cannot reveal. No amount of training fixes a missing input.

This is the honest ceiling of a track-only model, and the sharpest argument for the next step: a compact environmental-steering stream.
