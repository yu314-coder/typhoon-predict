Tip

Every full-horizon forecast this storm produced — 105 of them — drawn as one thin line each. Bold is the mean by valid time; dotted black is the observed track from genesis.

Coastlines are Natural Earth 50 m land polygons, simplified to the resolution each panel can actually show. Projection is equirectangular with longitude scaled by cos(latitude), so shapes are locally true at the centre of each panel.

Why the weighted consensus and not the plain mean. On Tip the equal-weight mean scores 267 km for v10, against 114 km for the weighted-and-smoothed consensus — better by about 58%. There is no case across the storms tested where the plain mean wins on position, so where only one line is drawn it is the weighted one; each caption still quotes what the mean would have scored.

Where the smoother is buying accuracy with shape. On Tip the v10 consensus turns 2.5° per step against 19.2° observed. The 2% rule still lands on heavy smoothing there, so the bold line is closer to the truth in position while being visibly straighter than the storm actually was. Read it as a position estimate, not as a description of how the storm curved.

The weighted consensus is additionally passed through a constant-velocity Kalman/RTS smoother. Solving every valid time independently makes the track jump whenever the short-lead membership rotates, producing turns no storm makes — on Co-may the point-wise solve swung 45° per step. The smoother treats each weighted position as an observation whose variance comes from the min-variance solve itself, so well-determined times pull hard and poorly-determined ones yield to the motion model. Its one parameter is chosen as the least smoothing whose error stays within 2% of the best, because minimising error alone flattens real curvature: a straight line through the middle of a turn scores well and is the wrong shape.

The lead-weighted consensus is an analysis, not a forecast. At each valid time the members come from different initialisations and so carry different lead times; the weights are minimum-variance on a Marchenko-Pastur-cleaned lead×lead error covariance, fitted leave-one-storm-out. Because the solution puts ~45% of its weight on leads 1–4 and none on leads 17–20, it leans on recent initialisations — so its error is not comparable to the 120 h forecast error beside it. Most of its advantage is simply lead-awareness; RMT only cleans the residual covariance shape.

The mean is averaged by valid time, not by lead. Forecasts launched hours apart only describe the same moment at different lead offsets — averaging by lead would blend positions hours apart and draw a route no model ever predicted.

The observed track is reconstructed from the base position of every window, which is an actual best-track fix, so it is data rather than a fit.
