v10 replaces the straight-line persistence baseline with one that extrapolates the storm's current turn rate. Here the grey (v9, straight) and teal (v10, curved) forecasts are drawn over the observed track. On the recurvers (Bavi, Co-may) v10 bends closer; on the true loopers (Hinnamnor, Wayne) both still run off — the loop needs steering data neither model has. The learned turn-extrapolation is small, so the two lines are often close.

What to look for. On Bavi and Co-may the teal (v10) line sits a little closer to the orange truth at long range — the curved baseline helps where the storm is already turning. On Hinnamnor and Wayne, both lines shoot past the loop: the reversal is driven by the steering environment, which best-track history cannot see, so no baseline shape recovers it.

Honest bottom line. The model learned only a tiny turn-extrapolation, so v10 ≈ v9 on aggregate. Direction changes are an information limit, not an architecture one — the ensemble spread is the honest way to flag "this one might turn."
